## Part 0: Executive Summary
### Executive Summary: Strategic Reconstruction of the 2026 Hormuz Crisis

*This section serves as a comprehensive standalone briefing. It quantifies the systemic transmission of a major supply-side shock across global asset classes and identifies exploitable market dislocations.*

---

### 1. Situational Context: Operation "Epic Fury"

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Timeline** | 47 days: February 28 – April 17, 2026 |
| **Catalyst** | Military engagement (Operation Epic Fury) closed the Strait of Hormuz — approximately **20% of global oil transit** |
| **Primary Shock Vector** | Physical supply disruption, not currency or financial contagion |
| **Analysis Objective** | Quantify the "War Premium," identify hedge failures, and isolate exploitable dislocations |

---

### 3. The Central Finding: Market Fragmentation

The Hormuz closure did not create a uniform "risk-off" environment. It **fragmented the market** into three distinct regimes:

| Regime | Assets | Behavior |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Physical Scarcity** | WTI Crude | Pure supply shock — uncorrelated with equities, dollar‑insensitive |
| **Liquidity‑Forced Liquidation** | Gold | Sold to meet margin calls — negative correlation with oil during stress |
| **Risk‑Off Contagion** | XOM, CVX, JETS | Tracked equity beta, not commodity fundamentals — amplification of losses |

**Quantified proof:** The equal‑weight Energy Basket (XOM + CVX + WTI) suffered a **−12.3%** max drawdown during closure — **4.5 percentage points deeper** than the S&P 500's −7.8%. An investor seeking "energy exposure" as a hedge would have been worse off than holding the broad market.

**Gold dislocation:** Gold dropped **−9.6%** during the closure window — the opposite of safe‑haven behavior. The WTI/Gold ratio rose +47%, confirming markets priced physical scarcity, not systemic fear.

---

### 4. International Spillover: The Oil Import Penalty

The shock transmission was **not uniform across geographies**. Oil‑importing economies absorbed significantly larger drawdowns:

| Market | Shock Window Return | Max Drawdown | Interpretation |
|--------|--------------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Nikkei 225 (Japan)** | +2.5% | **−12.1%** | Highest vulnerability — violent swings, full recovery |
| **DAX (Germany)** | **−2.0%** | **−9.5%** | Only negative return — industrial energy sensitivity |
| **S&P 500 (US)** | +2.3% | −7.8% | Most resilient — net exporter status |

**For USD‑based investors,** FX effects amplified these differences. DAX's USD‑adjusted loss widened to −3.9% in the reopen window as the dollar strengthened.

---

### 5. The Pair Trade That Worked: Long WTI / Short XOM

The decoupling between physical oil and energy equities created an **exploitable dislocation**:

| Window | Long WTI | Short XOM | Combined P&L |
|--------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Pre‑event | +16.9% | −25.2% | −8.2% (too early) |
| **Shock** | **+32.9%** | **+1.5%** | **+34.4%** |
| Reopen | +27.5% | −5.6% | +21.8% |

**Insight:** The market systematically mispriced energy equities during the acute shock. Shorting XOM against long WTI futures produced a +34.4% return in 33 trading days. This dislocation persisted through the reopening, suggesting structural rather than transient mispricing.

### 6. Volatility Regime: Contained at the Index Level, Explosive at the Commodity Level

| Asset | Peak 5d/21d Vol Ratio | Regime Classification |
|-------|----------------------|----------------------|

| **XOM** | 1.75 | Moderate — energy equity vol elevated but not extreme |

**VIX peaked at 31.0** — 1.5× the 5‑year average of 20.7. This is elevated but not panic territory.

**Critical takeaway:** Commodity volatility does not automatically translate to equity volatility. The financial system's plumbing remained functional throughout the 47‑day crisis.

---

### 7. Strategic Directives for Risk Managers

#### I. Treat Physical Oil and Energy Equities as Separate Asset Classes

Pre‑event beta for XOM was −0.02 (near zero). During shock, rolling beta flipped to **−0.42**. Energy equities became an *inverse* market position. Do not substitute XOM or CVX for WTI futures in hedging portfolios.

#### II. Monitor the WTI/Gold Ratio as a Regime Discriminator

- **Rising ratio (oil > gold)** → Supply shock dominant → Equities may decouple from commodity fundamentals
- **Falling ratio (gold > oil)** → Systemic fear dominant → Traditional hedges may resume functioning

The ratio rose **+47%** during closure — a clean signal that this was a scarcity event, not a flight‑to‑quality event.

#### III. International Exposure Requires FX Consideration

Nikkei's local‑currency return (+2.5%) hid a **−12.1%** drawdown. For USD‑based investors, DAX's reopen loss was −3.9% (versus −3.0% local). Hedge international equity exposure or accept that oil import vulnerability is asymmetric.

#### IV. The Reopening "Peace Signal" Was Priced in One Day

- WTI: **−11.5%** on April 17
- S&P 500: fully absorbed within **one trading day**
- JETS: +4.8% day‑1 recovery, but still ended reopen window **−10.7%**

Do not wait for equity market confirmation. WTI futures (24/5 trading) pre‑price equity opens. The futures open is the leading signal.

#### V. Correlation Regime Shift

The correlation between WTI and the S&P 500 flipped from **0.00 (neutral)** to **−0.58 (inverse)** during the blockade. Being "Long Oil" was not an equity hedge; it became a primary vector of portfolio risk that amplified total volatility rather than dampening it.

---

### 8. Market Efficiency & Resolution

The "Peace Signal" (April 17 Hormuz reopening) was absorbed with extreme efficiency:

| Asset | Day‑1 Reaction | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| **WTI** | **−11.5%** | Immediate evaporation of the war premium |
| **S&P 500** | **+1.2%** | Equities forward‑priced the macro improvement |
| **Airlines (JETS)** | **+4.8%** | Partial recovery, offsetting a −10.7% crisis‑window loss |

### 9. Methodological Limitations

| Limitation | Implication |
|------------|-------------|
| **Single event study** | Results describe 2026; may not generalize to shocks with different geopolitical topographies |
| **Daily data only** | Intraday lead‑lag dynamics invisible; CCF biased toward lag 0 |
| **Constant beta assumption** | Betas estimated on 39 pre‑event days; abnormal return magnitudes are directional, not precise |
| **No transaction costs** | All returns assume execution at quoted daily closes — pre‑cost upper bounds |
| **No CPI adjustment** | Multi‑year comparisons are nominal |

---

### Strategic Summary

> **Hormuz 2026 revealed a fragmented market structure:** physical oil decoupled from energy equities, gold failed as a safe haven, and oil‑importing economies absorbed asymmetric drawdowns.
>
> For risk managers, the actionable insights are:
> 1. **Long WTI / Short XOM** produced +34% during the shock — the decoupling was exploitable.
> 2. **VIX peaked at 31** — commodity chaos does not guarantee equity panic.
> 3. **The WTI/Gold ratio (+47%)** is the cleanest regime discriminator.
> 4. **Do not substitute energy equities for physical oil** in hedging portfolios.
>
> In high‑severity supply shocks, diversification into energy equities and gold provides no immediate drawdown protection due to systemic liquidity requirements and regime‑dependent correlation structures.

## Part 3: Event Window Analysis – What Happened When

The study divides the crisis into **three economically meaningful windows** based on the actual event timeline:

| Window          | Dates                  | Trading Days | Purpose |
|-----------------|------------------------|--------------|--------|
| **Pre-event**   | Jan 1 – Feb 27, 2026   | 39           | Establish normal relationships & betas |
| **Shock**       | Feb 28 – Apr 16, 2026  | 33           | Full blockade + peak war premium |
| **Reopen**      | Apr 17 – Apr 30, 2026  | 9            | Resolution & mean reversion |

**Why only three windows?**  
This structure captures the complete lifecycle of a geopolitical supply shock (anticipation → impact → resolution) while keeping the analysis clean and interpretable. More granular windows were considered but rejected — they add noise without meaningfully changing the key insights.

**Note on Apr 16/17 boundary:** The Lebanon ceasefire and Hormuz reopening declaration happened on consecutive days. We end the Shock window on Apr 16 (last full day of closure). This is a deliberate, transparent choice. Results are robust to small shifts in this cutoff.
**Important Note on the `Reopen` Window (Apr 17 – Apr 30):**

Although Iran officially announced the full reopening of the Strait of Hormuz on April 17, the move was **largely symbolic and short-lived**. Actual shipping traffic remained severely restricted due to the continued U.S. naval blockade on Iranian ports, Iranian threats to re-close the strait, and deep distrust among shipping companies and insurers.

As clearly visible in the **"2026 Jan-Apr: Normalised Adjusted-Close Paths (Base = 100 at Jan 1)"** chart:

- WTI crude experienced a sharp initial drop following the announcement (reflecting the immediate "peace signal").
- However, within roughly one day, the price began rebounding and by the end of April had climbed back close to its peak levels seen during the Shock window.

This reversal was driven by renewed geopolitical tensions, including:
- Continued U.S. and Israeli statements perceived as contradictory or insincere.
- Israeli military actions in Lebanon (including strikes on Beirut suburbs — the first since the Hezbollah ceasefire).
- Iran’s renewed support for its allies and threats to restrict the strait again.

**Conclusion:** This window should **not** be interpreted as a full or sustainable normalization. It instead captures a classic **"false dawn"** — an initial market relief rally quickly undermined by fragile geopolitics and lack of real de-escalation.

## Part 5: Abnormal Returns: Isolating the Hormuz Effect

A price move during a crisis can have two sources: (1) general market movement (beta), and (2) the specific shock. The market model separates them.

$$AR_{i,t} = R_{i,t} - (\alpha_i + \beta_i \cdot R_{m,t})$$

$$CAR_i(T_0, T_1) = \sum_{t=T_0}^{T_1} AR_{i,t}$$

Where:
- $R_{i,t}$ = asset return on day $t$
- $R_{m,t}$ = S&P 500 return on day $t$ (market benchmark)  
- $\alpha_i$, $\beta_i$ = OLS estimates from the pre-event window (Jan 1–Feb 27)
- $AR_{i,t}$ = abnormal return — the shock-specific component
- $CAR_i$ = cumulative abnormal return over a window

**Estimation window:** Jan 1 – Feb 27, 2026 (~39 trading days). Beta is estimated on pre-shock data to avoid contamination.

```python
# ── Bootstrap confidence intervals on shock-window abnormal returns ──────────
# Resample pre-event residuals 1000x to get SE on cumulative AR
np.random.seed(42)
N_BOOT = 1000
 
est_s, est_e = EVENT_CONFIG['estimation_window']
shock_s, shock_e = EW['shock']
est_rets = simple_returns(prices.loc[est_s:est_e]).dropna()
shock_p  = prices.loc[shock_s:shock_e]
 
boot_rows = []
for asset in [a for a in ['WTI','GOLD','XOM','CVX','JETS'] if a in prices.columns]:
    if asset not in est_rets.columns: continue
    bm = est_rets['SP500']
    ar = est_rets[asset].dropna()
    aligned = pd.concat([ar, bm], axis=1).dropna()
    if len(aligned) < 10: continue
 
    import statsmodels.api as sm
    model   = sm.OLS(aligned[asset], sm.add_constant(aligned['SP500'])).fit()
    resids  = model.resid.values
    n_shock = len(prices.loc[shock_s:shock_e].dropna(how='all'))
 
    # Bootstrap: resample residuals, sum over shock window length
    boot_cars = [resids[np.random.choice(len(resids), n_shock, replace=True)].sum() * 100
                 for _ in range(N_BOOT)]
 
    actual_ar = abnormal.loc[
        (abnormal['asset'] == asset) & (abnormal['window'] == 'shock'),
        'abnormal_ret_%'
    ]
    point_est = float(actual_ar.values[0]) if len(actual_ar) else np.nan
 
    boot_rows.append({
        'Asset':        LABELS.get(asset, asset),
        'Abnormal AR %': f'{point_est:+.1f}%',
        'Boot SE %':    f'{np.std(boot_cars):.1f}%',
        '95% CI':       f'[{np.percentile(boot_cars, 2.5):+.1f}%, {np.percentile(boot_cars, 97.5):+.1f}%]',
        'Signal/Noise': f'{abs(point_est) / np.std(boot_cars):.1f}x' if np.std(boot_cars) > 0 else 'N/A',
    })
 
boot_df = pd.DataFrame(boot_rows)
print('=== Bootstrapped Uncertainty on Shock-Window Abnormal Returns ===')
print('Method: 1000 resamples of pre-event OLS residuals, accumulated over shock window length\n')
display(boot_df)
note('Signal/Noise > 2x = abnormal return is statistically distinguishable from sampling noise. '
     'Values near 1x should be treated as directional only.')
```

## Part 7: DXY Decomposition: Was This Currency or Supply?

## Part 8: Lead-Lag: Does WTI Predict the S&P 500?

The cross-correlation function (CCF) tests whether WTI returns at time $t$ are correlated with S&P 500 returns at time $t+k$, for $k = -5$ to $+5$ days.
 
- **Positive lag** ($k > 0$): WTI moves first, S&P follows
- **Negative lag** ($k < 0$): S&P moves first, WTI follows  
- **Lag 0**: simultaneous movement
 
**Structural limitation upfront.** WTI (CME Globex) trades 24 hours, 5 days a week. The S&P 500 trades NY hours only. When WTI moves overnight, that information is already incorporated into equity prices at the next morning's open — but both close-to-close returns are recorded on the same calendar date. Daily CCF is therefore structurally biased toward finding peak correlation at lag 0, regardless of whether a true lead-lag exists. A proper lead-lag test would require intraday data (e.g., WTI close-to-open vs S&P open-to-close decomposition). This analysis cannot provide that with daily prices.
 
The CCF result here is best read as: *at daily frequency, there is no persistent multi-day predictive relationship between WTI and S&P 500 returns.* Whether WTI overnight moves lead equity opens within a single trading day is a separate question that daily data cannot answer.

The index's shock-window return of +2.5% masks this volatility: the Nikkei fell sharply mid-crisis, then recouped losses as the ceasefire announcement (April 7) signaled eventual resolution. This is the signature of a pure oil-importing economy — immediate earnings pressure on manufacturers (automotive, chemicals, electronics), followed by relief when supply normalizes.

#### DAX (Germany): Industrial Transmission

Germany, the second-largest oil importer in Europe, showed intermediate sensitivity with a **-9.5% maximum drawdown**. Unlike Japan, the DAX ended the shock window **down -2.0%** — the only negative return among the three indices studied.

2. **Proximity to geopolitical theater** — European markets priced broader escalation risk (NATO involvement, Mediterranean naval posture) that Asia-Pacific markets largely ignored.

The US became a net oil exporter in 2019, fundamentally altering its sensitivity to supply shocks. The S&P 500's **-7.8% maximum drawdown** was the shallowest of the three, and the index returned **+2.3%** during the 33-day closure.

The resilience reflects two structural factors:
- Energy sector weight in the S&P 500 is ~4% (versus ~15% in the 1980s). Technology and healthcare, which dominate the index, are not direct oil consumers.

The heatmap above is the analytical centerpiece of this study. Each cell represents the cumulative total return of one asset during one phase of the crisis. **2. Pre-Event (Jan 1 – Feb 27): Tension Was Already Priced**
- XOM (+25.2%), CVX (+21.0%), and Gold (+21.2%) all rallied materially before the formal blockade began.

**3. Shock Window (Feb 28 – Apr 16): The Decoupling Event**
- Only WTI (+32.9%) and the S&P 500 (+2.3%) posted positive returns.

### The Energy Hedge That Failed (During the Acute Crisis)

**Finding applies to the acute shock phase only (Feb 28 – Apr 16).**  
By May 2026, energy equities had reversed course and were outperforming both crude oil and the S&P 500. The analysis below describes the *crisis-period* relationship, not a permanent structural decoupling.

---

**During the 33-day closure window (Feb 28 – Apr 16):**

The equal-weight energy basket (XOM, CVX, WTI) recorded a maximum drawdown of **−12.3%**, versus **−7.8%** for the S&P 500 — a 4.5 percentage-point larger loss. An investor who allocated to energy exposure expecting portfolio protection during an acute oil supply shock would have been disappointed over this window.

**Why the basket failed during the crisis:**

| Component | Shock Window Return | Contribution to Basket |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------------|
| WTI Crude | **+32.9%** | Positive but insufficient |
| Exxon (XOM) | **−1.5%** | Dragged basket lower |
| Chevron (CVX) | **−0.8%** | Dragged basket lower |

Energy equities did not track crude prices upward during the shock. They were sold as risk assets when equity market sentiment deteriorated — disconnecting from the commodity they produce. XOM's maximum drawdown during closure reached **−13.1%**, deeper than the broad market's −7.8%.

---

**Important time‑qualification (added May 2026):**

The relationship inverted after the acute crisis subsided. By mid‑May 2026:

- The Energy sector (XLE) became the **best‑performing S&P 500 sector**, up ~14% year‑to‑date
- XOM and CVX **outperformed crude oil itself** over the same multi‑week period

The acute-phase decoupling (Feb–Apr) did **not** persist. Energy equities lagged crude during the shock but subsequently caught up and exceeded it as markets repriced balance sheet strength and capital return discipline.

---

**Risk management implication (qualified):**

| Time Horizon | Energy Equities as Oil Hedge? |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Acute shock (days to weeks) | **No** — they decoupled and amplified drawdowns |
| Multi‑quarter positioning | **Yes** — they ultimately tracked and exceeded crude |

A short‑term crisis hedge using energy equities failed in this event. A multi‑quarter energy allocation would have succeeded — but not as an intra‑crisis protection tool.

---

**The pair trade evidence:**

During the shock window, a hypothetical Long WTI / Short XOM position returned **+34.4%** (see chart below). This quantifies the decoupling: the market priced physical oil and oil equities as fundamentally different exposures during the acute phase.

*Caveats: No transaction costs, no short financing costs, no futures roll dynamics. Theoretical upper bound, not a trade recommendation.*

**The strategic insight:**

**The trade:** When a supply shock is confirmed (WTI/Gold ratio rising for 5+ consecutive days), execute:
- Long: Front-month WTI futures (or USO ETF as proxy)
- Short: XOM or XLE (energy sector ETF)

**Exit condition:** When the WTI/Gold ratio peaks and begins to reverse (supply normalization signal), close both legs. In 2026, the peak was April 7 (ceasefire announcement).

**Return from this strategy in 2026:** +34.4% over 33 trading days. Sharpe ratio not calculated due to vol, but directional effectiveness is unambiguous.

**Caveat:** This is a short-duration, high-conviction trade. Requires daily monitoring of WTI/Gold ratio and geopolitical news flow. Not a buy-and-hold strategy.

### Mandate 3: Gold Fails Under Margin Pressure — Do Not Blindly Buy Safe Havens

**What the data says:**
- Gold: **-9.6%** during closure
- First 5 days of shock: **-2.8%**
- Gold volatility: **34%** annualized (vs 16% historic average)

**The strategic insight:**

Gold dropped because institutions needed *liquidity*. They sold the asset with the largest paper gains (gold had rallied +21% pre-event) to meet margin calls on falling equity positions.

**The pattern to recognize:**

| Phase | Gold Behavior | What's Happening |
|-------|---------------|------------------|
| Pre-shock (tension building) | Rallies | Safe-haven buying |
| Early shock (panic, margin calls) | **Sells off** | Forced liquidation |
| Late shock (stabilization) | Recovers partially | Short covering |
| Resolution | Returns to normal correlation | Unwind |

**Risk rule:** Never buy gold *after* it has already rallied significantly into a crisis. The marginal buyer at that point is not a hedger — it's a future seller facing margin pressure. If you want gold exposure, enter during calm periods (VIX < 15, gold vol < 15%). During the acute shock phase, gold is a *source of liquidity*, not a destination for it.

**Alternative:** If you need a true safe haven during a supply shock, use **short-term Treasury bills (SHV, BIL)** . They do not face the same margin-driven liquidation pressure because their volatility is near zero and financing haircuts are minimal.

Oil needed time to verify shipping logistics, storage draws, and actual tanker movements. This creates a short window where oil is still volatile while equities have stabilized — an opportunity for mean-reversion trades.

**The macro implication:** A rising dollar during an oil shock is not a sign of US economic strength. It is a sign of **capital flight into USD-denominated assets** (Treasuries) as investors flee equity risk. The dollar becomes a *safe haven*, not a *policy signal*.

## Appendix: Methodology and Reproducibility
### A.1 Mathematical Framework
The analysis utilizes a standard event-study methodology to isolate shock-specific impacts from general market noise.

**Market Model (Abnormal Returns):**
To calculate the idiosyncratic impact of the crisis on specific assets:
$$AR_{i,t} = R_{i,t} - (\hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_i \cdot R_{m,t})$$
$$CAR_i(T_0, T_1) = \sum_{t=T_0}^{T_1} AR_{i,t}$$

**Supply-Side Decomposition:**
Isolating the physical supply shock from currency fluctuations:
$$WTI_{\text{shock}} = \beta_{DXY} \cdot DXY_{\text{shock}} + \epsilon_{\text{supply}}$$

**Volatility & Relative Value:**
*   **Volatility Regime:** $\text{Vol ratio}_t = \frac{\sigma_{5d,t}}{\sigma_{21d,t}}$ (Regime shift flagged if $> 2.0\times$).
*   **Commodity Spread:** $\text{Ratio}_t = \frac{P_{WTI,t}}{P_{GOLD,t}}$ (Rebased to 100 at Jan 1, 2026).

### A.3 Methodology & Operational Decisions
Technical choices made to ensure data integrity and minimize contamination.

*   **Return Type:** Adjusted Close (`auto_adjust=True`) to account for dividends and splits.
*   **Beta Estimation:** Derived via OLS using the **Pre-event window only** to avoid shock-induced volatility bias.
*   **Volatility Metric:** 5d/21d ratio utilized for interpretability over GARCH models.
*   **Benchmark Baseline:** 2021–2025 data serves as the historical control group.
*   **DXY Proxy:** ICE Futures (`DX-Y.NYB`) used as the primary liquidity proxy.

### A.4 Data Limitations & Constraints
*   **Timezone Displacement:** WTI/DXY close precedes US equity opens; Cross-Correlation Function (CCF) analysis is used to mitigate "phantom" same-day correlation.
*   **Survivorship Bias:** Energy sector baselines (XOM/CVX) do not account for delisted peers during the 5-year control period.
*   **Linearity Assumption:** Beta is estimated on a short window (~39 days); during extreme shocks, asset relationships may become non-linear.
*   **Nominal Values:** No inflation adjustments are applied to multi-year comparisons.
